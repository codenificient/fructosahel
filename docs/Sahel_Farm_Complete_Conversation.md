# 1. Rainwater-Harvested Potato Farming

## Question
For a profitable potato farm in the Sahel region, what is the minimum land area required to store enough rainwater to irrigate crops during the dry season?

## Key Assumptions

| Parameter | Value | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Annual rainfall | 300–500 mm (using 400 mm) | Concentrated in ~4 months (Jun–Sep) |
| Potato water demand | ~500–600 mm per crop cycle | ~100–120 day growing season |
| Irrigation efficiency | 85–90% (drip) | Furrow would be ~50–60%, not viable here |
| Catchment collection efficiency | 40–60% | Depends on surface treatment |
| Storage evaporation losses | 20–40% | Open reservoirs lose enormous amounts |

## The Math (for 1 hectare of potatoes)

**Water needed per hectare of crop:**
600 mm × 10,000 m² = **6,000 m³**, or ~6,667 m³ accounting for drip irrigation efficiency, and ~8,000–9,000 m³ after storage evaporation losses (assuming partially covered reservoir).

**Water collectible per hectare of catchment:**
400 mm rainfall × 50% collection efficiency = 200 mm = **2,000 m³/ha**

**Catchment area needed:**
~8,500 m³ ÷ 2,000 m³/ha ≈ **4–4.5 hectares of catchment per hectare of crop**

## Minimum Total Land Area

For a profitable operation (~1 hectare of potato cultivation, yielding 15–25 tonnes):

| Component | Area |
|---|---|
| Cultivated land | ~1 ha |
| Catchment surface | ~4–4.5 ha |
| Reservoir/storage footprint | ~0.3–0.5 ha |
| **Total minimum** | **~5.5–6 hectares** |

## Critical Considerations

**Storage is the hardest part.** An open earthen reservoir in the Sahel can lose 2,000+ mm/year to evaporation — potentially more than the entire rainfall collected. Investment in lined reservoirs with floating covers, or underground/covered cisterns is essential.

**Improving the ratio:**
- Treating catchment surfaces (compaction, plastic sheeting, concrete aprons) can push collection efficiency to 70–80%, reducing catchment area to ~3 ha
- Two shorter potato crop cycles timed around the wet/dry transition reduce the volume needed to store
- Supplementing with shallow groundwater wells can dramatically reduce the required catchment

**Profitability threshold:** At Sahelian market prices for potatoes (often $300–600/tonne), 1 hectare yielding ~20 tonnes generates ~$6,000–12,000 in revenue. The reservoir and drip system capital cost (~$5,000–15,000) typically pays back in 2–3 seasons — but only if storage losses are controlled.

**Bottom line:** Plan for roughly **5–6 hectares total** with proper infrastructure, or **~4 hectares** if catchment surfaces are optimized and covered storage is used.

# 2. Underground Reservoir + Processing Shed

## The Design Concept

Build an underground reservoir 2–3 meters deep, lined with concrete, with a processing facility shed built on top. Use solar pumps to irrigate.

## Why This Design Is Smart

- **Evaporation virtually eliminated.** Open reservoir loses 2,000–2,500 mm/year. Covered underground cistern drops losses to near zero.
- **Structural synergy.** The shed's foundation doubles as the reservoir walls — paying for concrete once and getting two structures.
- **Temperature control.** Underground water stays cool (25–30°C vs 40°C+). The shed benefits from thermal mass — thousands of cubic meters of water acting as a heat sink.
- **Revenue diversification.** A processing facility for multiple crops transforms the economics entirely.

## Sizing the Reservoir

For 1 hectare of irrigated potatoes (~6,000–7,000 m³ stored water):

| Depth | Required Footprint | Shed Dimensions |
|---|---|---|
| 2 m | ~3,500 m² | ~60 m × 60 m |
| 3 m | ~2,300 m² | ~48 m × 48 m |
| 2.5 m (compromise) | ~2,800 m² | ~53 m × 53 m |

**3-meter depth** is the sweet spot — footprint (~2,300 m²) is a large but realistic shed.

## Construction Considerations

- **Steel truss roof on concrete perimeter walls** — most practical for spans of 20–50 m with internal concrete pillars on a grid (every 6–8 m)
- **Reservoir floor:** Reinforced concrete slab, 150–200 mm thick, with waterproof membrane
- **Walls at 3 m depth:** 200–250 mm reinforced concrete minimum (hydrostatic pressure ~30 kN/m²)
- **Roof/floor slab:** 200–300 mm reinforced concrete to support processing equipment
- **Waterproofing:** Applied membrane (bituminous or HDPE) or crystalline waterproofing admixture

## Solar Pumping System

- Flow rate: ~5–10 m³/hour for 6–8 hours/day
- Pumping head: ~5–10 m
- Solar array: ~1–2 kW panel system
- Cost: $1,500–3,000 for complete solar pump kit
- No batteries needed if irrigating during daylight hours only

## Rough Cost Estimate

| Component | Estimate (USD) |
|---|---|
| Excavation (2,300 m³) | $5,000–10,000 |
| Reinforced concrete (floor, walls, roof slab) | $40,000–70,000 |
| Waterproofing membrane | $3,000–5,000 |
| Steel roof structure + cladding | $15,000–25,000 |
| Solar pump system | $2,000–3,000 |
| Drip irrigation (1 ha) | $1,500–3,000 |
| Catchment surface preparation | $2,000–5,000 |
| **Total** | **~$70,000–120,000** |

# 3. Drought-Resistant Companion Crops

## Cowpeas (Niébé) — The Ideal Partner

- **Nitrogen fixation:** 60–100 kg N/ha back into soil (potatoes extract 80–120 kg N/ha)
- **Deep taproots** break compacted subsoil, improving water infiltration
- **Drought tolerance:** Matures in 60–75 days, survives on 300 mm rainfall
- **Market value:** Staple protein across West Africa, stores well, processable

## Recommended Annual Rotation

| Season | Crop | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Dry season (Oct–Mar) | Potatoes (irrigated) | Cooler nights improve tuber formation |
| Wet season (Jun–Sep) | Cowpeas (rain-fed) | No irrigation needed, fixes nitrogen |
| Transition (Apr–May) | Incorporate cowpea residue | Green manure decomposes for potatoes |

## Strong Secondary Options

- **Bambara groundnut** — Even more drought-tolerant (200 mm rainfall), fixes nitrogen, premium prices
- **Sorghum / pearl millet** — Enormous biomass, root exudates suppress potato pathogens, useful every 3rd–4th cycle
- **Moringa** — Permanent perimeter planting, deep roots as nutrient pumps, leaf prunings as green manure, dried powder sells for $5–15/kg

## Three-Year Rotation for Maximum Soil Health

| Year | Dry Season (irrigated) | Wet Season (rain-fed) |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Potatoes | Cowpeas |
| 2 | Potatoes | Bambara groundnut |
| 3 | Sweet potatoes or onions | Sorghum/millet (biomass crop) |

# 4. The 20+ Hectare Integrated Farm

## Zone Layout (25 hectares)

| Zone | Area | Function |
|---|---|---|
| **Core** (Zone 0) | ~0.5 ha | Underground reservoir, processing shed, solar array, cold storage, residence |
| **Zone 1** — Intensive | ~3 ha | Market gardens, rice paddies, aquaculture ponds, duck/chicken runs |
| **Zone 2** — Semi-intensive | ~6 ha | Potatoes, onions, pineapples, rotational crops, sheep/pig paddocks |
| **Zone 3** — Extensive | ~8 ha | Food forest, fruit orchards, guinea fowl ranging, bee yards |
| **Zone 4** — Catchment & grazing | ~5 ha | Water harvesting, improved pasture, fodder crops, windbreaks |
| **Buffer perimeters** | ~2.5 ha | Moringa hedgerows, living fences, firebreaks |

## Water System

**Total rainfall capture: ~50,000 m³/year** (25 ha × 400 mm × 50% efficiency)

- **Underground reservoir:** 8,000–10,000 m³ under processing shed
- **Aquaculture ponds:** 3,000–4,000 m³ (productive water storage)
- **Rice paddies:** 2,000–3,000 m³ (wet season, natural fill)
- **Swales and infiltration basins:** Recharging groundwater across Zones 3–4

## Integrated Animal System

### Chickens (~200–300 birds)
Mobile coops rotated through crop fields after harvest. Scratch through residue, eat pest larvae, deposit manure. Eggs for daily income.

### Guinea Fowl (~100–150 birds)
Semi-free-ranging across Zones 2–3. Heat-tolerant, disease-resistant, eat ticks and grasshoppers. Premium meat prices across West Africa.

### Ducks (~50–100 birds)
Rice-duck system: ducks in paddies eat weeds, insects, snails. Paddling aerates roots. Manure fertilizes rice. Duck eggs at premium.

### Sheep (~30–50 head)
Rotational grazing in Zone 4. Sahel breeds (Peul, Oudah). Time breeding for Tabaski sales when prices spike 200–400%.

### Pigs (~20–30 head)
Processing waste recyclers (damaged potatoes, vegetable trimmings, fish offal). Manure for biogas. Cultural note: viable primarily in non-Muslim communities.

## Aquaculture — Tilapia Ponds

- 2–3 ponds, 0.3–0.5 ha total
- Target 3–5 tonnes/year
- Duck manure → algae → tilapia feed chain
- Pond water pumped for crop fertigation (free liquid fertilizer)
- Azolla surface culture fixes nitrogen, tilapia eat it, suppresses mosquitoes

## Crop Zones

### Zone 1 — Intensive Market Gardens (~3 ha)
- Rice paddies (1–1.5 ha): NERICA varieties, dry-season vegetables after harvest
- Market gardens (1–1.5 ha): Tomatoes, peppers, leafy greens, okra, herbs

### Zone 2 — Field Crops (~6 ha, rotational blocks)
- 4 blocks of 1.5 ha each on 4-year rotation
- Potatoes, onions, pineapple, sorghum/millet with cowpea/Bambara rotations

### Zone 3 — Food Forest & Orchards (~8 ha)
- **Overstory:** Baobab, néré, shea, balanites
- **Midstory:** Mango, cashew, papaya, guava, citrus
- **Understory:** Moringa, pigeon pea, cassava, sweet potatoes
- **Ground layer:** Peanuts, cowpeas, leguminous cover crops

## Solar Infrastructure

- **Total demand:** ~15–20 kW
- **Array:** ~120 m² of panels on shed roof
- **Cost:** ~$15,000–25,000 with battery storage

## Nutrient Cycle

```
Crop residues → Animal feed → Manure → Compost → Soil → Crops
Processing waste → Pigs → Biogas → Energy
Fish pond sediment → Compost → Food forest
Duck manure → Pond algae → Tilapia feed
Pond water → Fertigation → Market gardens
Moringa prunings → Green manure + Animal feed + Product
Azolla → Tilapia feed + Rice paddy N-fixation + Pig supplement
```

## Phased Implementation

- **Year 2:** Complete shed, 4 ha crops, guinea fowl, ducks, rice paddy, second pond, sheep
- **Year 3:** Full rotation, all animals, processing facility operational

## Ferrocement Tank Construction Summary

### Phase-by-Phase Build (28 days per tank)
1. **Foundation (Days 1–3):** 100mm reinforced concrete slab, 2–3% slope to center drain
2. **Armature (Days 4–5):** Rebar cage + double-layer chicken wire mesh
3. **Plastering (Days 6–9):** Rich 1:2.5 mortar, interior skim coat with waterproofing
4. **Curing (Days 10–24):** Minimum 14 days continuously wet — critical in Sahel heat
5. **Testing (Days 25–28):** Fill test, pH check, flush 2–3 times before stocking
